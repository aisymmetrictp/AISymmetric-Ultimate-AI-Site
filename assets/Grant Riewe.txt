About
The work I find most interesting sits where complex technology and organizational reality converge — where the architecture decision and the people decision turn out to be the same decision. Most technology failures aren't technical. They're failures of translation: between what's possible and what the organization can absorb, between what the strategy assumes and what execution actually requires. I've spent twenty years on that gap, in environments where the cost of getting it wrong was high.

As Chief Technology Officer of Vibrant Emotional Health, I built the technology and the organization — from a team of 30 to 140 people, $75M budget, national infrastructure connecting 200+ crisis centers — while the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline went live and was routing millions of contacts. Before that, nearly 15 years in total at McKinsey and Accenture, running digital transformations for Fortune 500 companies in consumer, retail, and healthcare. I deployed conversational AI solutions in brand-critical customer experiences before large language models existed — which meant solving the hard version of the problem without the velocity and scale the current wave takes for granted.

I serve on the board of the Alaska Whale Foundation, which conducts field research on humpback behavior and marine ecosystem health in Southeast Alaska — work that moves slowly, requires patience, and matters over decades rather than quarters. I taught emerging technology strategy at the University of St. Thomas, where I found that explaining something to a graduate student is one of the better ways to find out whether you actually understand it. I also build things with wood, which turns out to be good training for knowing the difference between a structure that looks solid and one that is.
